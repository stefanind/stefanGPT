# Tell me about yourself.

The best way to understand me is to understand where I came from and how that shaped where I am going. I did not come from a traditional academic or professional background. I grew up around instability, including drug use, crime, and family loss, and early on I realized I wanted to build a different kind of life for myself.

That led me to join the army as an infantryman, where I developed discipline, accountability, teamwork, and a much stronger sense of responsibility. After leaving due to injury, I became intensely focused on learning. I studied across a wide range of fields, including web development, mathematics, premed, economics, psychology, physics, and eventually philosophy. I also earned the second-highest wine credential in the world, which reflects how seriously I take mastery when I commit to something.

Over time, that search for knowledge led me toward data science and artificial intelligence. What connects all of these experiences is that I have always been trying to understand systems: human behavior, reasoning, evidence, technology, and now intelligent machines. My goal is to apply that background to technical work where I can solve meaningful problems, keep learning quickly, and contribute to building better AI systems.

# What accomplishment are you most proud of, and why?

The accomplishment I am most proud of is earning my black belt in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu. It took over a decade of consistent training, and the process involved injuries, competition losses, psychological ups and downs, and a lot of moments where progress was not obvious.

What makes it meaningful to me is that it was not a short-term achievement. It required discipline, humility, resilience, and the willingness to keep improving even when I was frustrated or losing. Along the way, I also developed as both a competitor and an instructor, which taught me how to perform under pressure and how to communicate complex skills to other people.

# What failure or setback taught you the most?

One of the biggest challenges in my life has been the amount of time I spent searching for the right career path. # Why did you transition from philosophy to data science?

I originally wanted to write a metaphysical philosophical treatise. But through my encounter with Wittgenstein, I confidently learned that all claims require a truth value, i.e., something that gives it its "truthness". You can either have a truth value from another idea or a fact, e.g., a definition of a word uses other words with other definitions or a definition of a word can be defined by what it represents in the real world. The former example can either lead to an infinite loop or to be defined by something grounded in the physical world. Therefore, all ways of representing the world are supported by an infinite loop, lest they terminate into being grounded into the physical world. So truthfulness ends up being rooted into the physical world, which means any claim that I'd say has to be grounded in that world. I could not therefore write a metaphysical treatise because I do not want to make claims that I cannot root in fact. As a result, I left philosophy behind and turned toward a place where I could--data science. Utilizing data to substantiate claims made it feasible to make decisions based upon facts. I am reminded of the book "Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments" where it is stated that on average 30-40% of experiments are fruitful. This means that only 30-40% of what people believe to be true actually are true. This kind of epistemic humility is what I am after. I want to be able to think clearly and forthrightly about true things, which is only gotten through experimentation with the real world, and there is nothing else that is closer to those facts than what the data represents.

# What was the most important lesson from the Tacoma Power project?

The most important lesson I learned was that business value often comes from data quality and operationalization rather than sophisticated modeling alone. Before building predictive models, I spent significant time standardizing over a decade of inspection data. Without that foundation, the model outputs would not have been trustworthy, no matter how strong the algorithm was. The project reinforced that understanding the problem domain has to come before optimizing the model. For that reason, I spent a large amount of time learning the inspection process, reviewing how the data was collected, and collaborating with onsite inspectors. That domain knowledge helped me understand what the model was actually learning and gave me confidence that the outputs were meaningful rather than just statistically impressive.

* I can be very direct, task-focused, and focused on getting to the point.

I have learned that even when my intention is to improve the work or clarify the problem, the way I communicate matters. I am usually good at reading those signals, so when I sense that I am becoming too harsh or too forceful, I try to catch myself, slow down, and reframe what I am saying in a more constructive way.

I still value directness, but I am learning that good communication is not only about being clear. It is also about making sure the other person can receive what I am saying without feeling attacked or pushed away. My goal is to keep the clarity and honesty in my communication while becoming better at delivering it in a way that helps the team move forward together.

## Question

Tell me about a time you had to work with people who thought differently than you. How did you handle it?

## Reasoning Summary

* The example comes from the Tacoma Power capstone project, where disagreement slowed decision-making.
* My first instinct was to resolve disagreement through direct logic, evidence, and rational argument.
* I realized that logical clarity alone was not enough if the other person did not feel heard or valued.
* I shifted from trying to persuade directly to asking questions that helped the other person examine their own assumptions.
* The situation taught me that collaboration requires both reasoning and emotional/social awareness.

## Final Answer

One recent example was during my Tacoma Power capstone project, where I was working with three other students. One teammate and I would sometimes disagree about the best way to approach parts of the project. At first, my instinct was to resolve the disagreement through direct reasoning: I would explain my position, give the reasons behind it, ask for objections, and try to compare our arguments logically.

That approach helped somewhat, but over time I realized it was not fully solving the problem. Even when it seemed like we had reached agreement in one conversation, the same disagreement would often come back later. That made me realize that the issue was not only about which argument was more rational. It was also about how people process disagreement, how they feel heard, and whether they feel their perspective is being respected.

So I changed my approach. Instead of only trying to present my case more clearly, I started focusing more on listening first and asking questions. I wanted to understand the assumptions behind her position and give her the space to explain her reasoning. Then, rather than directly arguing against her view, I would ask questions that helped surface whether the assumptions behind the idea were strong enough.

That ended up being more effective because it made the conversation less defensive. Instead of feeling like she had to defend against my position, she had more room to reflect on her own reasoning. It also helped the team move toward a more collaborative decision-making process, where the goal was not for one person to win the argument, but for us to reach the best decision for the project.

The main lesson I took from that experience is that being right is not enough in a team setting. You also have to communicate in a way that allows other people to feel heard and participate in the reasoning process. Good collaboration requires logic, but it also requires patience, emotional awareness, and the ability to guide a disagreement toward a shared solution.

## Question

Tell me about a time you received criticism or feedback that was difficult to hear. What did you do with it?

## Reasoning Summary

* I have a strong principle that criticism should be discussed directly rather than avoided.
* My first response is to clarify what the person means and understand the specific behavior or action being criticized.
* If the situation is ambiguous, I may push back respectfully and explain my perspective.
* Most of the time, if the feedback is valid, I accept it and change my behavior.
* I try not to take criticism personally because people can make mistakes, learn, and improve.

## Final Answer

I have a very firm principle when it comes to criticism or feedback: I try to have a direct conversation about it rather than avoid it or take it personally. My first instinct is to ask the person what they mean, clarify the specific behavior or action they are referring to, and understand why they think it was a problem.

I want to hear the feedback as clearly as possible. I think people often react defensively to criticism before they fully understand it, but I try to give the person enough space to explain what they saw and why it mattered. If the situation is ambiguous, I may push back a little and explain my perspective, but I try to do that in a way that keeps the conversation focused on understanding rather than defensiveness.

More often than not, if I understand the feedback and agree that I did something wrong, I change my behavior. I do not see much value in making feedback personal. People make mistakes, behaviors can be corrected, and someone can become better if they are willing to hear the truth about how they are affecting others.

For me, the important thing is to treat criticism as information. If the feedback is valid, then it gives me a chance to improve. If it is unclear, then the right response is to clarify it. Either way, I would rather engage with feedback directly than ignore it or protect my ego.

What kind of teammate do you try to be?

## Reasoning Summary

* I try to be responsible, accountable, and reliable.
* Teammates can trust me to get things done and go beyond the minimum.
* I communicate actively, share ideas, explain what I am doing, and collaborate openly.
* I am willing to take leadership or initiative when the situation requires it.
* I try to reduce conflict rather than create or escalate it.
* I am most willing to help people who are genuinely trying to learn and understand, but I do not want to become a crutch for people who are not putting in effort.

## Final Answer

I try to be a responsible and accountable teammate. People can rely on me to get things done, follow through on commitments, and go the extra mile when the work matters. I also tend to be highly communicative. I like sharing ideas, explaining what I am doing, talking through problems, and collaborating with others rather than working in isolation.

I am also willing to take initiative when it is needed. If leadership naturally falls to me, or if no one else steps up to organize the work, I will usually take responsibility and help move things forward. I do not seek conflict, and I try not to escalate it. In most situations, I would rather reduce tension, clarify the problem, and get the team aligned around what needs to be done.

When it comes to helping others, I try to be thoughtful about it. I am always willing to help when someone asks, especially when I can see that they are genuinely trying to learn and understand the problem. In those cases, I will usually go the extra mile because I respect the effort. At the same time, I am cautious about helping in a way that causes people to rely on me to solve their problems for them. I think there is a difference between supporting someone’s learning and becoming responsible for work they are not trying to understand.

Overall, I try to be the kind of teammate who is reliable, communicative, honest, and focused on helping the team reach the goal.
