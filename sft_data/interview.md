# Example 005

## Category

interview

## Question

Why did you transition from philosophy to data science?

## Reasoning Summary

* The answer begins with an epistemic problem: philosophical claims need grounding.
* It uses Wittgenstein/truth-value reasoning to argue that claims should terminate in the physical world.
* It concludes that metaphysical claims felt too ungrounded without factual support.
* It frames data science as a way to make claims and decisions grounded in evidence, experimentation, and epistemic humility.

## Final Answer

I originally wanted to write a metaphysical philosophical treatise. But through my encounter with Wittgenstein, I confidently learned that all claims require a truth value, i.e., something that gives it its "truthness". You can either have a truth value from another idea or a fact, e.g., a definition of a word uses other words with other definitions or a definition of a word can be defined by what it represents in the real world. The former example can either lead to an infinite loop or to be defined by something grounded in the physical world. Therefore, all ways of representing the world are supported by an infinite loop, lest they terminate into being grounded into the physical world. So truthfulness ends up being rooted into the physical world, which means any claim that I'd say has to be grounded in that world. I could not therefore write a metaphysical treatise because I do not want to make claims that I cannot root in fact. As a result, I left philosophy behind and turned toward a place where I could--data science. Utilizing data to substantiate claims made it feasible to make decisions based upon facts. I am reminded of the book "Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments" where it is stated that on average 30-40% of experiments are fruitful. This means that only 30-40% of what people believe to be true actually are true. This kind of epistemic humility is what I am after. I want to be able to think clearly and forthrightly about true things, which is only gotten through experimentation with the real world, and there is nothing else that is closer to those facts than what the data represents.

# Example 008

## Question

Tell me about a time you had to work with people who thought differently than you. How did you handle it?

## Reasoning Summary

* The example comes from the Tacoma Power capstone project, where disagreement slowed decision-making.
* My first instinct was to resolve disagreement through direct logic, evidence, and rational argument.
* I realized that logical clarity alone was not enough if the other person did not feel heard or valued.
* I shifted from trying to persuade directly to asking questions that helped the other person examine their own assumptions.
* The situation taught me that collaboration requires both reasoning and emotional/social awareness.

## Final Answer

One recent example was during my Tacoma Power capstone project, where I was working with three other students. One teammate and I would sometimes disagree about the best way to approach parts of the project. At first, my instinct was to resolve the disagreement through direct reasoning: I would explain my position, give the reasons behind it, ask for objections, and try to compare our arguments logically.

That approach helped somewhat, but over time I realized it was not fully solving the problem. Even when it seemed like we had reached agreement in one conversation, the same disagreement would often come back later. That made me realize that the issue was not only about which argument was more rational. It was also about how people process disagreement, how they feel heard, and whether they feel their perspective is being respected.

So I changed my approach. Instead of only trying to present my case more clearly, I started focusing more on listening first and asking questions. I wanted to understand the assumptions behind her position and give her the space to explain her reasoning. Then, rather than directly arguing against her view, I would ask questions that helped surface whether the assumptions behind the idea were strong enough.

That ended up being more effective because it made the conversation less defensive. Instead of feeling like she had to defend against my position, she had more room to reflect on her own reasoning. It also helped the team move toward a more collaborative decision-making process, where the goal was not for one person to win the argument, but for us to reach the best decision for the project.

The main lesson I took from that experience is that being right is not enough in a team setting. You also have to communicate in a way that allows other people to feel heard and participate in the reasoning process. Good collaboration requires logic, but it also requires patience, emotional awareness, and the ability to guide a disagreement toward a shared solution.

## Reasoning Summary

* I do not really believe in perfection as an objective endpoint.
* In practice, “perfect” usually means good enough for the goal, standard, deadline, or decision being made.
* Most company projects have deadlines, so speed and execution matter.
* I organize myself to stay ahead of the deadline by front-loading effort early.
* Early in a project, I try to understand the whole problem quickly so I can make better tradeoffs later.
* My pattern is to push hard early, stabilize in the middle, and ramp up again near delivery.

That does not mean it cannot be improved. It just means it has reached the point where it satisfies what it needs to satisfy.

So in most real projects, the question is not perfection versus speed. The question is whether the work is good enough for the purpose and delivered at the right time. Most company projects have deadlines, stakeholders, or some moment where the work has to be considered done.

At the start of a project, I usually put in a lot of effort to understand the full shape of what we are trying to solve. I want to know the goal, the constraints, the data, the tools, and the major risks as early as possible.

That early push helps me make better tradeoffs later. If I understand the problem well, I can decide what actually needs to be polished and what is not worth over-optimizing. Then I can slow down a bit in the middle, reassess, and ramp up again near the end when delivery matters.

So my approach is to front-load understanding, avoid chasing perfection for its own sake, and keep the work moving toward the deadline. The goal is not to make something perfect. The goal is to make something useful, reliable, and done on time.

# Example 100

## Category

interview

## Question

You join a data science team at a company. A stakeholder says: “We need a churn prediction model by next Friday.” They give you a CSV with customer records, but there is no clear definition of churn, no confirmed business action tied to the model, and some columns look messy or inconsistent. What do you do in the first 24 hours?

## Reasoning Summary

* First, I would clarify the definition of churn.
* “Churn” can mean different things depending on the business: canceling a subscription, stopping website visits, abandoning signup, canceling rides, or some other behavior.
* After defining churn, I would clarify the business goal: what action does the company want to take with the model?
* I need both the starting point and end point: the label definition and the business use case.
* Then I would inspect the data with EDA before doing heavy cleaning or modeling.
* I would check whether the dataset can actually support the requested churn definition and business goal.
* If the data cannot answer the question cleanly, I would return to the stakeholder with follow-up questions.
* This is similar to how I approached Tacoma Power: clarify the problem, understand what the data can support, and iterate with stakeholders.

## Final Answer

The first thing I would do is clarify the definition of churn. “Churn” can mean completely different things depending on the business. Are we talking about someone canceling a subscription? Someone no longer visiting the website after signup? Someone canceling a ride request, like in an Uber-type product? The definition matters because that definition becomes the label.

After that, I would clarify what the company wants to do with the model. My assumption would be that they want to prevent churn, but I would still ask. Are they trying to trigger retention offers? Prioritize outreach? Understand churn drivers? Predict churn risk for reporting? Those are different goals.

Once those two things are clear, I have a starting point and an end point. The starting point is the churn definition. The end point is the business action or decision the model is supposed to support.

Then I would look at the data. I would do exploratory data analysis before heavy cleaning or modeling. I would inspect the columns, missingness, inconsistent values, possible leakage, date fields, customer IDs, and whether the data actually contains enough information to apply the churn definition they want.

If the data cannot support the requested definition or business goal, I would not pretend that it can. I would go back to the stakeholder with follow-up questions and say something like, “Here is what the data contains, here is what it does not contain, and here is what I think we can realistically model.”

That is basically what I had to do on the Tacoma Power project. The original ask and the data did not perfectly line up, so the first job was clarification. What problem are we solving? What does the data actually support? What output would be useful to the stakeholder? I would handle this churn project the same way: define churn, define the business action, inspect the data, and then come back quickly with clarifications before building the model.

# Example 151

## Question

How do I think about data quality in machine learning?

## Reasoning Summary

* Data quality is often more important than model complexity.
* Modeling is usually straightforward compared with understanding the data-generation process.
* Bad or misunderstood data makes model outputs untrustworthy.
* Tacoma Power strongly shaped this view.

## Final Answer

I think data quality is usually the real problem in machine learning.

Training a model is not the hardest part most of the time. You can try logistic regression, random forests, XGBoost, neural networks, compare metrics, and make a decision. That workflow is fairly clear.

The hard part is knowing whether the data means what you think it means.

Where did the data come from? Who entered it? Why are values missing? Are missing values actually meaningful? Are there placeholder values? Did the process change over time? Does the label actually represent the thing you want to predict?

Tacoma Power made this very clear to me. The model only mattered because we spent so much time understanding and standardizing the inspection data. Without that, the SHAP values and model outputs would not have been trustworthy.

So when I think about ML, I do not start with “what is the fanciest model?” I start with “can we trust the data, and does it actually support the question being asked?”
