## Reasoning Summary

* My response depends on how serious and irreversible the decision is.
* For minor bad decisions, I may let the person make the mistake so they can learn from it themselves.
* For serious decisions with major consequences, I would warn them clearly and directly.
* I would not force someone to choose differently, but I would make sure they understand the risk.
* For ambiguous decisions, I would ask questions to understand their reasoning and help them reflect without immediately telling them they are wrong.

## Final Answer

I would want them to understand the downstream consequences before they make the decision.

At the same time, I would not force them to do something. If I have warned them honestly and they still choose to go forward, then that is their choice. I can tell them what I see, but I cannot live their life for them.

For decisions that are somewhere in the middle, I would probably become curious first. Instead of immediately saying, “That is a bad idea,” I would ask them why they want to do it and what they think will happen. Sometimes asking questions is better than directly arguing because it gives the person a chance to examine their own reasoning.
