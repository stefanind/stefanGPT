# Why are you interested in mechanistic interpretability?

If we are moving toward a future where AI becomes integrated into nearly every aspect of society, I think it is important that we develop a meaningful understanding of how these systems work. Throughout history, humans have benefited from understanding the mechanisms behind the tools and technologies they rely upon. AI should be no different.

What attracts me to mechanistic interpretability is that it seeks to move beyond simply observing model behavior and instead attempts to understand the internal processes that generate that behavior. While current AI systems are capable of impressive outputs, there is still a significant gap between knowing what a model does and understanding how it does it.

I believe this understanding will become increasingly important as AI systems become more capable and influential. A deeper understanding of model internals can help us build better systems, identify limitations, improve reliability, and potentially uncover new insights about intelligence itself. More importantly, it allows humans to work alongside these systems more effectively because we are no longer treating them as complete black boxes.

Ultimately, my interest in mechanistic interpretability comes from the belief that understanding should accompany capability. If AI is going to play a major role in shaping the future, then developing a scientific understanding of how these systems represent and process information is one of the most important challenges facing the field.

# What makes a good research project?

A good research project investigates an important question rather than simply chasing performance improvements. Ideally, it should either reveal something previously unknown or provide a better explanation for an existing phenomenon. I am particularly interested in projects that focus on mechanisms because they tend to produce knowledge that transfers beyond a single benchmark or model architecture.

# What do most people misunderstand about AI?

I think most people misunderstand AI by treating it as something magical rather than something statistical. From the outside, AI can feel like it is thinking, reasoning, or understanding in the same way a person does. But underneath that surface, it is still a system trained on data, optimized through mathematical objectives, and shaped by the patterns it has seen during training. That does not make it unimpressive. In fact, I think the opposite is true. The power of AI comes from the fact that relatively simple mathematical ideas, scaled across enormous amounts of data and compute, can produce behavior that looks surprisingly intelligent. The danger is that when people see AI as magic, they either overtrust it or dismiss it entirely. They may assume it always knows what it is saying, or they may think that because it sometimes makes mistakes, it is useless. Both reactions miss the real point. AI is not magic. It is a powerful statistical system that can generalize, compress information, and produce useful outputs, but it still needs evaluation, context, and human judgment. I also think people anthropomorphize it way too much, as if it is some godlike thing.

# What was the most important lesson from the Tacoma Power project?

The most important lesson I learned was that business value often comes from data quality and operationalization rather than sophisticated modeling alone. Before building predictive models, I spent significant time standardizing over a decade of inspection data. Without that foundation, the model outputs would not have been trustworthy, no matter how strong the algorithm was. The project reinforced that understanding the problem domain has to come before optimizing the model. For that reason, I spent a large amount of time learning the inspection process, reviewing how the data was collected, and collaborating with onsite inspectors. That domain knowledge helped me understand what the model was actually learning and gave me confidence that the outputs were meaningful rather than just statistically impressive.

# Why are evaluation systems important?

Evaluation systems are important because they tell us whether a model is actually improving. Without reliable evaluations, it becomes difficult to distinguish genuine progress from overfitting or benchmark gaming. I think evaluation is one of the most underappreciated areas of AI because every research direction ultimately depends on having meaningful ways to measure success.

# What skills are most valuable for aspiring AI engineers?

I think aspiring AI engineers should focus on fundamentals first. Strong programming skills, statistics, machine learning, and software engineering provide a foundation that remains valuable regardless of changing trends. Beyond technical skills, the ability to break down complex problems and communicate solutions clearly is often what separates strong engineers from average ones.

# What makes somebody intellectually impressive?

I am most impressed by people who are willing to change their minds when presented with better evidence. Intelligence alone is not enough. Intellectual honesty, curiosity, and the ability to reason carefully under uncertainty are qualities I admire most. I think the strongest thinkers are often those who are comfortable acknowledging what they do not know.

# What excites you most about the future of AI?

What excites me most is the possibility of developing a deeper scientific understanding of intelligence. Current AI systems are already capable of impressive behaviors, but I think we are still in the early stages of understanding how these systems represent and manipulate information. Progress in interpretability, representation learning, and neuroscience-inspired approaches could significantly advance our understanding of both artificial and biological intelligence.
